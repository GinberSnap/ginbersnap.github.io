Learning Objectives, Bloom's Taxonomy, & SMEs (SAM)

Learning Objectives


Overview of the differences between course learning outcomes and learning objectives

The key difference between Course Learning Outcomes and learning objectives lies in their scope and focus. While Course Learning Outcomes define expected achievement upon completion of an entire course, learning objectives or module learning objectives (MLOs) provide clear and measurable target goals that students are expected to meet by a lesson or module.

Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs)

Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs), terminal outcomes or course goals are the overcall goals of an entire course. CLOs define the specific knowledge, skills and abilities that students are expected to gain upon completing the course. CLOs include the following.

  • Guidance for Design: They provide clear direction for the course and help clarify its overall goals and purpose.
  • Alignment: CLOs assist instructional designers in creating well-aligned learning experiences that connect objectives, teaching methods, and assessments.
  • Assessment and Evaluation: They outline the criteria for what students are expected to achieve.

CLO Examples

The following CLOs are terminal outcomes because they represent the highest level of the overall course goals and demonstrate the ability to apply cryptographic principles to the design of secure systems in realistic ethical hacking scenarios.

CLO Example 1: By the end of the course, learners will conduct a penetration testing exercise focused on a range of cryptographic weaknesses, thoroughly detailing their findings and proposing strategic remediation measures.

CLO Example 2: By the end of this course, learners will be able to successfully decrypt and analyze intercepted ciphertext encrypted with common symmetric and asymmetric algorithms, demonstrating their understanding of the underlying cryptographic principles and identifying potential vulnerabilities.

Learning objectives (MLOs, WLOs)

Learning objectives allow instructional designers effectively break down course objectives and create building blocks into measurable steps. This helps instructional designers break down course-level goals into achievable module-level objectives.

MLOs Examples

Module 1: Topic - Introduction to Ethical Hacking
- Module Learning Objective: By the end of this module, learners will be able to compare ethical hacking and malicious hacking practices.

Module 2: Topic - Legal Landscape of Ethical Hacking
- By the end of this module, learners will be able to analyze the potential legal and professional consequences of unauthorized hacking actions.

Bloom's Taxonomy

Overview

Bloom's Taxonomy was developed by Benjamin Bloom in 1956 (University of Florida, n.d.). This fundamental framework categorizes educational objectives and cognitive skills into 6 levels of learning, providing a hierarchical structure to guide curriculum development and assessment.

(1) Remembering: Recalling or simply memorizing facts, terms and concepts as face value.

(2) Understanding: Making sense of facts, terms, and concepts and convey underlying meaning.

(3) Applying: Apply acquired knowledge to solve problems in new and practical situation.

(4) Analyzing: Breaking down information into its component parts to examine overall structure and relationships, using skills like comparison and differentiation.

(5) Evaluating: Able to make well-informed judgments or assessments based on evidence and criteria.

(6) Creating: Develop original concepts, outputs, or methods by applying their acquired knowledge
