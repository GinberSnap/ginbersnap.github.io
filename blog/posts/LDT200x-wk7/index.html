Learning Objectives

Overview of the differences between course learning outcomes and learning objectives

The key difference between Course Learning Outcomes and Learning Objectives lies in their scope and focus. While Course Learning Outcomes define expected achievement upon completion of an entire course, Learning Objectives or Module Learning Objectives (MLOs) provide clear and measurable target goals that students are expected to meet by the end of each lesson or module.

Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs)

Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs), Terminal Outcomes or Course Goals are the overcall goals of an entire course. CLOs define the specific knowledge, skills and abilities that students are expected to gain upon completing the course. CLOs include the following.

  • Guidance for Design: It provides clear direction for the course and help clarify its overall goals and purpose.
  • Alignment: CLOs assist instructional designers in creating well-aligned learning experiences that connect objectives, teaching methods, and assessments.
  • Assessment and Evaluation: They outline the criteria for what students are expected to achieve.

CLO Examples

  • CLO Example 1: By the end of the course, learners will execute penetration tests and formulate strategic remediation measures.
  • CLO Example 2: Upon completing the course, learners will demonstrate their understanding of common symmetric and asymmetric algorithms and decrypt encrypted ciphertext using common cipher tools.

    • Learning objectives (MLOs, WLOs)

    Learning Objectives allow instructional designers to break down course-level goals into clear, actionable steps at the module level.

    MLOs Examples

    • Module 1 (MLO1): Introduction to Ethical Hacking
      By the end of this module, learners will distinguish between ethical and malicious practices, articulating key differences in purpose and legality. Successful completion of MLO1 will enable learners to meet CLO1.
    • Module 2 (MLO2): Legal Landscape of Ethical Hacking
      By the end of this module, learners will be able to analyze the potential legal and professional consequences of unauthorized hacking actions. Successful completion of MLO2 will enable learners to meet CLO2.

    Bloom's Taxonomy

    Overview

    Bloom's Taxonomy was developed by Benjamin Bloom in 1956 (University of Florida, n.d.). This fundamental framework categorizes educational objectives and cognitive skills into 6 levels of learning, providing a hierarchical structure to guide curriculum development and assessment.

    1. Remembering: Recalling or simply memorizing facts, terms and concepts as face value.
    2. Understanding: Making sense of facts, terms, and concepts and convey underlying meaning.
    3. Applying: Apply acquired knowledge to solve problems in new and practical situation.
    4. Analyzing: Breaking down information into its component parts to examine overall structure and relationships, using skills like comparison and differentiation.
    5. Evaluating: Able to make well-informed judgments or assessments based on evidence and criteria.
    6. Creating: Develop original concepts, outputs, or methods by applying their acquired knowledge


    Reference: University of Florida (n.d.). Bloom's Taxonomy. https://citt.ufl.edu/resources/the-learning-process/designing-the-learning-experience/blooms-taxonomy/
