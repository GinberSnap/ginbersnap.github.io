Dick and Carey model

Overview of the Dick and Carey model


The Dick and Carey model was developed by Walter Dick and Lou Carey in 1978 (Hostos Community College, n.d.). One of its key characteristics is the use of "chunking" when designing learning programs. Chunking involves dividing programs into smaller, more manageable units to make learning objectives easier to achieve and measure. While the ADDIE model tends to follow a linear, sequential process, the Dick and Carey model emphasizes a structured, learner-centered, and outcome-driven approach.

Ten Steps of Dick and Carey Model

1. Identify the instructional goals: Clearly define the objectives and outcomes that students should achieve after completing the learning program.

2. Conduct instructional analysis: Analyze the overall instructional components, including training materials, learning environment, and learning goals.

3. Identify Entry Behaviors (analyze learners): Identify the learner’s entry behaviors or starting point, including learner characteristics such as prior knowledge, skill level, learning preferences, and technical competencies.

4. Write performance objectives: Set clear and measurable goals that describe what the students will be able to demonstrate at the end of the program.

5. Develop assessment instruments: Design assessment methods that meet the instructional objectives while assessing the student’s progress.

6. Develop instructional strategy: Establish instructional strategies that help guide the students throughout the learning program.

7. Develop and select instructional materials: Create learning materials and select instructional resources that support instructional strategies and help students successfully achieve the learning objectives.

8. Design and conduct formative evaluation: Perform a formative evaluation to assess the effectiveness and quality of the instructional materials based on feedback, while making continuous improvements.

9. Revise Instruction: Use student evaluations and feedback to make necessary revisions and improvements to the instructional resources and curriculum.

10. Conduct Summative Evaluation: Conduct a summative evaluation to measure the program's effectiveness and confirm that it achieves the intended instructional objectives and goals.


Implications of the Dick and Carey model


The Dick and Carey model would be suitable for my minicourse as my course will include hands-on technical activities. The "chunking" method of the Dick and Carey model breaks down technical instructions into more manageable, engaging, step-by-step activities. This makes each unit easier for students to follow and simplifying otherwise complex tasks. The Dick and Carey model will also help maintaining a logical flow and the relationship between each unit.


Strengths and limitations of the Dick and Carey Model


Strength: With the Dick and Carey model, the learning program is divided into smaller units to make them more manageable. Each unit is carefully analyzed and has clear, measurable objectives. These units are interconnected, ensuring that the entire program aligns with the overall goals.

Weakness: While the Dick and Carey model focuses on flexibility and iterative design, the process requires significant resources and time. In addition, it can add complexity due to its ten-step process. In some cases, the Dick and Carey model may not work well for programs because it is too prescriptive.
