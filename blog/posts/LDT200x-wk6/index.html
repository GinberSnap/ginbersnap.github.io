Successive Approximation Model (SAM)

Successive Approximation Model (SAM) was developed by Michael W. Allen that focus on rapid development. Unlike other traditional models, such as ADDIE, SAM is agile and responsive to meet frequent changes and prioritize collaboration and flexibility.

Key iterative phases in SAM

Preparation: instructional designers work closely with stakeholders and subject matter experts (SMEs) during the initial preparation phase. The key goals at this stage include identifying project objectives, understanding the target audience, and evaluating available resources. A detailed project blueprint is then created to define the overall scope and strategic direction.

a. Project initiation
b. Needs assessment
c. Defining learning objectives
d. Background information gathering
e. Stakeholder collaboration

Iterative Design: In the second phase, a prototype or "Alpha" version of the course materials is developed. This version emphasizes content structure, instructional methods, and the overall learning experience. Instructional designers use it to gather feedback from stakeholders and subject matter experts (SMEs).

a. Savvy start
b. Project planning
c. Design elements
d. Prototype development
e. Formative evaluation
f. Feedback analysis

Iterative Development: Instructional designers refine and improve learning materials based on feedback they receive in the previous phase. In addition, multimedia and visual elements are added to the course contents to improve engagement, reinforce key concepts, and enhance the overall learning experience. This iterative process continues until the materials align with the intended learning goals and objectives.

a. Design proof
b. Alpha
c. Beta
d. Gold
e. Rollout
