Understanding by Design (UbD) Model

Overview


Understanding by Design (UbD) is a curriculum and instructional design framework that follows the backward design approach. Developed by Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe in 1998, it consists of three stages: identifying desired learning outcomes, designing assessments, and planning instructional activities..

1. Identify Desired Results:
Identify Desired Results is the first stage in the Understanding by Design (UbD) framework, where clear learning objectives and outcomes are established at the beginning of the process. In this stage, curriculum goals or expectations are defined and set priorities, while addressing key questions, such as "What should students know, understand, and be able to do?" (McTighe & Wiggins, n.d.).

2. Determine Acceptable Evidence:
In the Determine Acceptable Evidence stage, measurable outcomes are identified to assess student learning progress. Assessments such as essays, peer reviews, and performance tasks help improve student understanding. For instance, a step-by-step, hands-on exercise on cryptography could involve students analyzing encryption algorithms to assess their understanding of security concepts.

3. Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction:
The Plan Learning Experiences and Instruction stage focuses on creating the learning experiences.
This includes designing activities and training materials that align with curriculum objectives, ensuring students achieve the desired learning goals.

Implications of the Understanding by Design (UbD) model


The Understanding by Design (UbD) model is as commonly used as the ADDIE and Dick and Carey models. While ADDIE and Dick and Carey focus on systematic, structured methods, the UbD model's backward design approach has significant implications for curriculum planning. Learning objectives and expected outcomes are clearly defined first, making the learning program more concept-driven. UbD also helps in creating measurable assessments to track students' achievement to meet the desired learning outcomes.

Strengths and limitations of the UbD Model


Strengths: The UbD model’s backward design approach ensures that curriculum and learning activities, including assessments, quizzes, and discussions, are developed based on clearly defined learning objectives. This method helps promote student understanding rather than simple memorization or "surface-level learning". Additionally, the UbD model offers flexibility and adaptability, allowing learning activities to be customized based on students' specific needs and requirements.

Limitations: The UbD model can be complex and time-consuming as it requires significant planning and resources. Designing assessments that meet the course objectives requires careful alignment with learning objectives, backward planning, and continuous improvement. Especially, it may also be difficult for new educators to implement due to their limited experience or if adequate resources, such as curriculum guides and professional development, are not available.
